Number two. The house of cards begins.

Here's what nobody knew: from day one, FTX and Alameda Research were essentially the same company with two different names. The numbers are staggering. By early twenty twenty-two, Alameda had borrowed over eight billion dollars directly from FTX customer deposits. Number four. The seventy-two hour collapse.

Everything unraveled in November twenty twenty-two, and the speed was unprecedented in financial history. On November second, CoinDesk published a leaked balance sheet showing that Alameda Research's assets were primarily FTT tokens, which were essentially IOUs created by FTX itself. Changpeng Zhao, the CEO of Binance and Sam's biggest competitor, saw the report and announced that Binance would be selling their entire position of five hundred million dollars worth of FTT tokens. This single tweet triggered a death spiral that destroyed both companies within seventy-two hours.

Here's the timeline: November sixth, customers requested over six billion dollars in withdrawals from FTX. November seventh, Sam tweeted that FTX had a liquidity crunch but was processing all withdrawals. This was a complete lie. Internal messages show that Sam knew FTX was missing at least eight billion dollars in customer money and had zero ability to honor withdrawal requests.

November eighth, Binance briefly considered acquiring FTX to save customers, but after examining the books for just six hours, they walked away. Changpeng Zhao said the hole in FTX's balance sheet was too large and the misuse of customer funds was too severe to fix.

November eleventh, FTX filed for bankruptcy. Sam resigned as CEO, but the damage was done. Over one million customers had lost access to their funds, and the total shortfall was estimated at over eight billion dollars.

Number five. Justice served.

The legal reckoning was swift and decisive. In December twenty twenty-two, Sam was arrested at his Bahamas compound and extradited to the United States. Federal prosecutors charged him with seven counts of fraud and conspiracy, each carrying potential sentences of over ten years in prison.

The evidence was overwhelming. Prosecutors presented bank records, internal communications, and software code that proved Sam had been stealing customer funds since twenty twenty. The jury deliberated for just four hours before returning guilty verdicts on all seven counts.
